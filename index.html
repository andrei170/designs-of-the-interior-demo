The Firm

A quarter-century studio that the AI layer cannot describe.

Designs of the Interior was founded in 1999. The studio has grown into a 12-person team handling full-service residential interiors and hospitality projects across northeast Wisconsin and the South Carolina coast. The work is documented, the team is real, and the portfolio is broad enough to anchor a regional reputation. None of that is reflected in how AI engines currently answer questions about luxury interior design in Green Bay, Door County, or Hilton Head.

This audit is a one-time pass at five queries a serious prospective client would actually run before reaching out. The pattern was consistent: smaller, newer, or out-of-market firms surface; DOTI does not. That is a structured-data and authority-signal problem, not a craft problem.

Three-Region Portfolio

The work spans three distinct design markets.

Each region has its own buyer, its own search intent, and its own competitive set. None of the three currently surface DOTI in an AI answer for the obvious queries.

Region 01

Green Bay Whole-Home Residential

Full-service interior design for high-end primary residences across the Green Bay metro and surrounding NE Wisconsin communities. The home market is where DOTI's 27-year reputation was built and where most local referrals still originate.

Region 02

Door County Lake Homes & Hospitality

Second-home and resort-tier projects on the Door County peninsula. The Door County buyer is searching from outside the region (Milwaukee, Chicago, Minneapolis) and increasingly starting that search inside an AI assistant rather than Google.

Region 03

Hilton Head, SC Coastal Portfolio

South Carolina coastal residential and hospitality work. A Wisconsin-headquartered firm with a documented Hilton Head portfolio is an unusual and defensible position; today, AI answers treat that geography as if DOTI does not work there at all.

What Would Actually Change This

A structured-data and content build the engines can read.

The work below is what closes the gap between the firm DOTI is and the firm the AI layer currently describes. Nothing here changes the studio's craft or brand presentation. It changes what the machines can see.

01

Firm-Level Schema

Embedded Organization and InteriorDesigner schema declaring foundingDate "1999-01-01", numberOfEmployees, and ownership. The 27-year tenure becomes a fact the engines can quote, not a tagline they have to infer.

02

People Schema

Person schema for Stephanie Bruss as Principal, plus structured records for senior designers and project managers. AI answers about "who runs DOTI" and "the designers at DOTI" become anchored to verifiable individuals.

03

Residential Pillar Page

A dedicated residential pillar covering whole-home, kitchen, bath, and new-build interior design with project metadata. Resolves "luxury interior designer Green Bay" and the long-tail residential queries that flow out of it.

04

Hospitality Pillar Page

A second pillar specifically for restaurant, boutique-hotel, and clubhouse interiors. Hospitality buyers search differently from homeowners; one combined page underperforms two focused ones in both classical SEO and AI retrieval.

05

Multi-Region Service-Area Structure

Distinct service-area URLs and LocalBusiness records for Green Bay, Door County, and Hilton Head, each with portfolio cross-links and region-appropriate FAQ. The Hilton Head footprint stops being a hidden asset.

06

FAQPage Layer

FAQPage schema answering the actual top-of-funnel questions: process, timeline, scope, regions covered, and how a Wisconsin firm runs South Carolina projects. Direct AI-retrieval fuel.

Why This Window Matters

The luxury and hospitality buyer is already on the AI side of the line.

The first-touch behavior for high-end residential and hospitality work has moved. A relocating second-home buyer asking ChatGPT for a Door County designer, or a restaurant group asking Perplexity for a Wisconsin hospitality designer, never reaches a page-one Google result. They take the answer the engine gives them and contact the three names it offered.

DOTI's 27-year independent track record, 12-person studio, three-region portfolio, and Hilton Head footprint are exactly the kind of facts these systems prefer to cite, once they can read them. Right now they cannot. The fix is mechanical, not creative. It is also the kind of structured build that compounds, because the same schema and pillar architecture keeps paying down through every new project page added on top of it.

The competitive window is the period before the rest of the market does this work. In luxury interior design, that window is still open.

Common Questions

Four questions before any conversation.

How is this different from SEO?

Classical SEO optimizes for Google's ten blue links. This work optimizes for the way ChatGPT, Perplexity, Claude, Gemini, and Google AI Overviews retrieve and cite sources. The two overlap on technical hygiene but diverge sharply on structured data and entity-level authority signals. A site can rank well on Google and still be invisible to AI engines, which is roughly the position DOTI is in today.

Does any of this touch the public brand or visual identity?

No. The build sits underneath the site as schema, structured content, and additional pillar pages. The visual brand, photography direction, and tone DOTI has built over 27 years stay exactly where they are. The goal is to make those assets legible to machines, not to redesign them.

What about the Hilton Head portfolio specifically?

The Hilton Head work is the single most underleveraged asset in the audit. A Wisconsin-headquartered firm with a documented South Carolina coastal portfolio is an unusual and defensible position, and zero of five engines currently surface it. A dedicated Hilton Head service-area page with portfolio cross-links and proper LocalBusiness schema is the highest-leverage piece of the build.

What is the next step if this is interesting?

A 30-minute conversation, no deliverable attached. The aim is to walk through the audit table together, confirm the residential-versus-hospitality split, and decide whether the multi-region structure described above matches how Stephanie wants the firm represented going forward. From there, scope and sequencing are straightforward to talk through.
